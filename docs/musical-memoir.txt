at my public school, 6th grade was the first year we had the opportunity to
participate in special music classes: band, choir, or orchestra. so near the
end of 5th grade during our general music class, we had the opportunity to try
some instruments before choosing what class and instrument we wanted to start
learning.

my first choice was to try the bassoon (_fagotto_ in italian; though i didnt
know it yet) but they didnt have an actual bassoon there that day, just a
reed. the teacher offered to let me blow on the reed, but i didnt because that
didnt seem very interesting to me. so i walked over the orchestra section,
because i had thought about the violin. i tried the violin, and then the
orchestra teacher said, “how about the viola”…i guess i must have tried and i
just nodded and that is how i started playing the viola in sixth grade.

we had always listened to music in the car, but i hadnt really ever listened
to it on my own until probably around 7th grade, when i got a computer in my
room. those were the days of napster. i remember listening to “joga” by bjork,
“kashmir” by led zeppelin. i got into black sabbath, and i really liked the
first system of a down album. (i really do still like it, actually, even
though im rarely in the mood. in the summer of 2016 i was a summer camp
instructor for 4-6 years old…it *really* did not suit me and one week i played
that album on the way to camp each day…my coinstructors riding in the van with
me were…disturbed? reasonably)

i was in orchestra during all this time and i enjoyed it, but didnt really
know that much about classical music at that point. sometime during 7th or 8th
grade i read cry to heaven by anne rice, which really moved me in some ways,
including stiffly, but it also got me interested in opera. i cant remember
exactly which was my first opera. it was either a vhs of don giovanni at la
scala with thomas allen and riccardo muti, or it was rinaldo (1711) by handel
with david daniels, cecilia bartoli, and bernarda fink. rinaldo was what
really got me though, because cry to heaven was about the castrati, which if
youve never heard of that (i apologize, in advance):

around the year 1600 the pope decided that women could no longer speak in
church…or sing in church. choral music at the time was very important in
church services, and only having low voices would not do. there was some
effort to import spanish falsettists, but then someone hit on a brilliant
idea. they realized that all the prepubescent boys, who had soprano voices,
would be just about trained by the time their voices changed…so, maybe, what
if, say, the boy “fell off a horse” and the only way we could save his life
was to cut his balls off…or maybe the horse stomped on them. hence, castrati,
though at the time they were more often known as i musici. despite the
procedure, the boys body wasnt entirely locked in a state of youth; sometimes
the voice changed anyway, or became ugly where it was beautiful before. but,
in enough cases, it succeeded, and the church now had “men” who could sing in
the alto and soprano range. eventually the castrati came to be used outside
the church, particularly on the stage in opera.

handel used many castrati in his operas. rinaldo has four roles for male
characters who sing in an alto or mezzo-soprano register. i was quite taken by
this and wanted to become a countertenor, as a male falsettist is called
today. i even took singing lessons at one point during high school. i sang o
mio babbino caro at my first recital, then a bass aria from the magic flute, o
isis und osiris schenket at my second and final voice recital. i really wanted
to be an opera conductor, but that didnt work out.

the practice of making castrati for the church continued until about 1860,
even though they had fallen out fashion for opera by the late 1700s — mozart
wrote for castrati; i dont believe that donizetti, bellini, rossini, or other
such composers did. the last castrato, alessandro moreschi, died in 1922. he
was a singer in the sistine chapel, and in 1902 and 1904 make a few solo
recordings on some of the earliest recording equipment. this is him singing
bach/gounoud’s ave maria

my immersion in rinaldo and don giovanni led me deeply into the world of opera
in particular, and classical music in general. i became very passionate in
some ways about playing. i say in some ways because i didnt always practice as
i should have. i was caught up much more in the idea and romance of it than i
was in the actual work necessary to do it well…as was a theme for me for a
long time.

i took private lessons, played in a local youth orchestra, and even entered
into the so-called young artists competition that was held by the orchestra. i
submitted a recording, playing the piece lachrymae by benajamin britten, based
on a song of john downland, for viola and piano. i was selected to compete at
the live competition, one of six. i did not win, got sixth…apparently my c
string was out tune (good thing it wasnt my g string!), which will be relevant
later.

at this point i was seriously considering going to music school. i planned to
do viola performance, but really wanted to be a conductor, preferably an opera
conductor. i visited bard college, the new school in nyc, the new england
conservatory in boston. i applied and auditioned at iu jacobs school of music,
nec, and mcgill university in montreal. i was accepted at iu and mcgill, and
chose to go to iu.

my first semester of college was quite intense. i got to play in the orchestra
for an opera, verdi’s rigoletto, which i enjoyed, but near the end of the
semester it just wasnt feeling right. i told me teacher that i was thinking
about quitting, and he said, playing the viola is kind of like being married;
that decided me! gtfo. it was a really good decision for me, undoubtedly the
right one, for many reasons, but there is one i want to focus one:

the whole time i played the viola, there was one thing about it in particular
that i just couldnt get, i just couldnt do right. that was: to play in tune.
even to tune my instrument! i knew the principle of tuning my instrument and
did it more or less, but i was never confident in it. in orchestra the oboe
plays a4=440hz, and we tune our ‘a’ string, the highest string on the viola,
to that pitch from the oboe, and from that we turn all the other strings, in
“perfect” fifths…or at least some kind of fifths.

i was never ever confident that my ‘a’ string was correctly tuned. and it
seemed like it should be easy…just match the note! its the same note! how hard
could it be!?! well it was quite difficult for me, and then i could get the
rest of my strings mostly in tune, but if someone had told they werent in
tune, i wouldve automatically believed them because i had no faith in my
ability to tune my instrument.

now i must have played mostly in tune, because i got into a competition, and i
got into college, and i was often second chair in the viola section, but it
wasnt because i could hear myself playing in tune or not…i basically played by
sight; i.e., when i looked at the music i knew where my hand and fingers were
supposed to go, and i mostly got it right i guess. i could not audiate, i
could not hear the music “in my head”. if i look at music, i can tell you the
name of the notes, but i have *no idea* how they sound just by looking at
them.

what is going on? am i just tone deaf?

it turns out i am not tone deaf and there is actually a deep reason for all of
these things.

so i left the music school and continued my life, eventually getting a
bullshit in mathematics and then moving to washington state and then living in
a van in portland…yeah i was pretty close to the river (and had a weird crush
on the fremont bridge)…and then eventually moved back to live my dad in time
for the…events…of 2020.

i studied sanskrit quite intensively for about a year, and then just couldnt
anymore.

i was browsing youtube one day and saw a photo of a boy, a musician…yeah i
clicked because i wanted to look at him, and i did; not only is he very cute,
his is also a beautiful person, and i sort of fell in love with
him…anyway/also part of the reason that i sort of fell in love with him was
that he showed me that i wasnt tone deaf. it wasnt my fault! it was the fault
of temperament. jacob showed me this and it changed my life.

jacob did a cover version of the song moon river, all acapella, all his own
voice; he won a grammy for it.1

jacob uses apple logic pro to record and mix his music, and he has done a
number of what he calls logic session breakdowns, where he takes a song, opens
up the logic session, and goes section by section, track by track, showing how
he recorded this particular sound and what effect he added there, etc.;
brilliant and entertaining

so for moon river, since he was using his own voice, he could tune it how he
wanted, since he wasnt confined to the 12-tone equal temperament system of
fixed pitch instruments (well…not like that really confines him anyway…). he
talks about this during the breakdown, but there is one point where he shows
it to. if you want to see, go to the time 1:04:00 and watch for about three to
five minutes; the truly magical moment happened for me right around 1:05:55,
though the context starting from 1:05:30 is essential

at 1:05:30 he starts playing a justly tuned c-major triad on his digital
organ, which is what he was explaining (somewhat confusingly) at that point.
at about 1:05:53 he turns off the justly tuned part and says “this is the
piano” meaning that the triad the organ is playing at that point is the way a
standard 12tet piano sounds, an equal-tempered c-major triad

the magic is, i heard the difference! i heard the difference! i was listening
to that justly tuned triad for about 20 seconds before he played the equal-
tempered triad and in that moment it was obvious! it was so obvious that they
were different! and if i could hear that, it meant i wasnt tone deaf! and, im
not…i am temperament deaf, though. 12tet just makes no sense to my ears.
chords are incomprehensible to me. (that is, aurally. i understand the
theory…i got an a+ in my college music theory class because it was all
written)

this revelation brought up a lot of grief in me, and a sense of betrayal.
whenever my music teachers would tell me i wasnt playing in tune i would ask
them how to know if i was playing in tune, how to learn to play in tune. they
usually said, use a tuner, which knows how to tune things to 12tet, or use a
piano, which is tuned in 12tet.

at the time, i didnt know what 12tet was. i thought that the way a piano was
tuned was some sort of god given inevitability; there were twelve notes and
those notes were the ones on the piano and my intonation should be exactly
like a pianos. no one ever said this explicitly, so perhaps there was a
misunderstanding on my part…but neither did anyone explain about tuning
systems.

i felt betrayed because i thought it was my fault that i couldnt hear in tune,
but the word “temperament” in “12-tone equal temperament” literally means that
its not in tune. yes, most of the music of the western world is actually not
in tune. it turns out my ears and my brain do not do well with equal
temperament. those just intonation chords just sound so good to me, and the
12tet so bad.

by the way, jacob has ridiculous ears. he has “perfect pitch”, which is most
likely a form of pitch memory, though, like most people his perfect pitch is
going flat. his ‘a’ isnt 440hz anymore…i think somewhat recently i heard him
say his ‘a’ - meaning if he just sings an ‘a’ from his pitch memory, it turns
out to be around 434hz. but his ears and brain are so sensitive to sound…by
they way, vedic astrology tangent, id love to see his birth chart for all the
musical karma indicators; he just has such a musical talent that i can only
imagine that he has worked on it for lifetimes…but he does crazy things like
split an octave on demand into 30 equal parts. i.e., he sings a chromatic
scale in 30-tone equal temperament with no reference. he also has crazy good
rhythm…[he does a polyrhythm 2:3:4:5:6 using all five fingers of one
hand](https://youtube.com/shorts/9Jua53-w4U4?si=C7-2XcIAVVFJBgyt). is this
what you would call being a fanboy? its not unhealthy, really
