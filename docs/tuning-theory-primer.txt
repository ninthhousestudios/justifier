#### tuning theory

after that catharsis let us learn some pleasant sound arithmetic.

the basis of tuning theory derives from the natural phenomena of the way some
things vibrate, specifically the way our voices vibrate. we are going to use
the example of a string, because it is simpler to visualize. i find it very
magical, partly in the sense that it is hard to believe. sometimes it is hard
for me to believe that what i am about to tell you is true, but it is. it has
been demonstrated scientifically and is not merely magic.

so, let us take a string. now, in the real world there are many factors and
complications, but right now we are only considering the fundamentals of what
is happening, so this is how it works in an ideal world.

so, we take a string and pluck it. the whole string vibrates, i.e., the string
vibrates as a whole and this vibration creates a sound that we can visualize
as a sine wave

[![](https://substack-post-
media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/8d9e3796-a3d2-4c24-9b6d-5a83f4cb956e_640x358.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gwAO!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-
post-
media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8d9e3796-a3d2-4c24-9b6d-5a83f4cb956e_640x358.png)

it goes up and down. the number of times is goes up and down over a certain
period of time is called the frequency. usually we use the unit hertz, Hz. 1
Hz is one cycle per second. in the case of our sine wave, one cycle is the
time it takes to go from one crest to another. so if we have a frequency at
100Hz, there are 100 cycles in one second.

so let us say that the string as a whole vibrates 100 times a second, so the
frequency that sounds is the frequency of 100Hz.

here is the magic: **at the precise same time** as the whole string is
vibrating, each half of the string is also vibrating; i.e., each half of the
string is vibrating independently of the whole string. each half of the string
vibrates **twice** as fast as the string as a whole. so if the whole string
vibrates at 100Hz, each half vibrates at 200Hz.

now, again, **at the same time** as this is happening, each third of the
string is also vibrating. each third of the string vibrates three times as
fast as the whole string, so each third of the string produces at tone at
300Hz.

this continues up to the point where it cannot physically do so. each fourth
of the string vibrates, producing a tone at 400Hz, each fifth vibrates
producing at tone at 500Hz, etc. here is a gif illustrating this

[![](https://substack-post-
media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/f1cb4abd-f9cd-4b3e-b13b-c20b0f09c564_640x300.gif)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!z5kF!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-

now, when we pluck this string, the tone we hear is said to have a frequency
of 100Hz, which is called the fundamental. the fundamental is the frequency
that the string itself is tuned to. all of these other tones that are produced
as called **upper partial tones** , because they are tones above the
fundamental, and they form a part of the sound. they are more commonly known
as harmonics, and sometimes as overtones.

[![](https://substack-post-
media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/b83d877f-6d85-4d3c-8e60-eb27f829d1f8_800x761.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!epaH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-

our vocal cords vibrate in this way and there are types of singing, called
overtone singing, that allow a person to selectively emphasize a certain upper
partial tone, which are often very high and sound “whistlely”. the overtone
you hear is there if they sing the same pitch in their regular voice, but it
just isn’t very obvious, because all of the upper partial tones blend together
to create the sound you hear. in overtone singing, they are able to make one
overtone stand out more than the others.

so all of these harmonics are present whenever we sing a note, or when a
string vibrates, either by being plucked or with a bow. sometimes these upper
partial tones can be distinguished by themselves, depending on the instrument,
the situation, and the person who is listening, but usually we simply hear
their combination, which is heard to have the frequency of the fundamental. so
the string in our example is heard to have the pitch 100Hz, even though there
are many of frequencies occurring at the same time.

different instruments have different timbres, which basically just means they
sound different. the reason why is complex, but one important aspect to this
is the overtones they produce. most instruments vibrate in such a way to
produce harmonic upper partial tones as just described, i.e., their upper
partial tones vibrate at frequencies that are integer multiples of the
fundamental. but different instruments may have different intensities of
certain harmonics. that is, some instruments might make more of the even
harmonics, some instruments might have more pronounced odd harmonics. this is
an important aspect to the quality of the sound they produce.

it is also a likely reason why i had trouble tuning my ‘a’ string. you might
remember that in orchestra the oboe would play a4=440hz, and i would need to
tune my viola’s ‘a’ string to that pitch…and i always had such trouble, and i
always thought it meant i was tone deaf. but, the timbre of the oboe is very
different from that of the viola, i.e., they sound different, duh!, but more
specifically they produce different levels of certain harmonics. my hypothesis
is that this is what made it so difficult. it is possible that my ears are
more sensitive to overtones than they are to fundamentals2, and therefore it
would have been very confusing to them to match a fundamental when the
harmonics of the two pitches were so different; that is, the harmonics
themselves are not different, but certain harmonics are emphasized in an oboe,
and certain others are more important in forming the sound of the viola. im
not sure exactly how to test this hypothesis, but i hope someday to have the
chance.

this physical fact of vibratory nature and the resultant set of overtones is
called the harmonic series, which is the basis for just intonation, and the
perversion of which forms the foundation for 12-tone equal temperament.

#### intonation

what is intonation, exactly?

let us take the pitch 236Hz; is this pitch in tune?

(take a moment to consider; listen if you would like to this aria from handels
rinaldo, “sulla ruota di fortuna”, sung by countertenor daniel taylor)

so, is 236Hz in tune? sorry, trick question.

the question doesnt make any sense because one pitch by itself cannot be in
tune or out of tune. a pitch can only be in tune or out of tune relative to
another pitch.

so to decide if a pitch is in tune, we need to compare it to another pitch. we
can compare the frequencies of two pitches by showing them as a ratio, with
one number on top of another, such as ³⁄₁. this is not a fraction, because we
are not breaking anything apart here. we are comparing two pitches.

since Hz is a measure of how often something vibrates in one second, if both
of the numbers in this example are in the units Hz, then 300Hz/100Hz means
that the upper pitch vibrates 300 times a second and the lower pitch vibrates
100 times a second. we can reduce this ratio and cancel the unit, giving 3/1,
which indicates that the higher pitch vibrates three times as fast as the
lower pitch. said differently, every time the lower pitch vibrates once, the
higher pitch vibrates three times.

in the most strict definition of just intonation, as long as two pitches can
be related to each other using whole numbers, then those pitches are in tune.
however, there is a general rule: the lower the numbers involved the more
subjectively “in tune” the interval will sound; that is, lower numbers will
sound better, higher numbers sound worse, when the ratio is reduced.3 for
instance, according to our definition, the interval 236Hz/235Hz is in tune,
but the numbers are very high and they probably wouldn’t sound very good
together.

so how does this form this basis of just intonation in a practical sense?

let us go back to the harmonic series. the fundamental pitch we will call _f_.
as we noted the second harmonic vibrates twice as fast as the fundamental, so
the second harmonic vibrates at a rate of 2 _f_. so the ratio between the
second harmonic and fundamental is 2 _f/f_ = 2/1.

if we sounded at pitch at 100Hz and one at 200Hz, you would notice something
interesting. the two pitches are “the same” in some sense. obviously they are
different pitches, but they share some kind of identity with each other,
something that neither share with, say, 150Hz.

we say that 200Hz is an octave above 100Hz. an octave of a note is a note that
is basically the same note, just higher or lower. to go an octave above a
pitch, we multiply the frequency by 2, as we saw above. since 100Hz is an
octave below 200Hz, we can also go down an octave by dividing the frequency of
a pitch by 2.

for example, if we want to go an octave below 100Hz, we divide by 2, which is
the same as multiplying by 1/24. so the pitch an octave below 100Hz is
100Hz*(1/2) = 50Hz.

what about an octave above 200Hz? 200Hz*2 = 400Hz. how does this relate to our
fundamental _f_ =100Hz? 400Hz/100Hz = 4/1. so the pitch 400Hz is two octaves
above the fundamental at 100Hz. in fact, this is the fourth harmonic, because
the fourth harmonic is produced by one fourth of a string vibrating four times
as fast as the fundamental.

octaves are great, but as you can see they are basically “the same” note. how
do we get a different note? lets look at the third and fifth harmonics.

the third harmonic vibrates at 3 times the rate of the fundamental. the fifth
harmonic vibrates as 5 times the fundamental. i hope you get the pattern now.
the _n_ th fundamental vibrates at _n_ times the rate of the fundamental, and
the number _n_ is always an integer, meaning it is always a positive whole
number.

if _f_ =100Hz, then 3 _f_ =300Hz and 5 _f_ =500Hz. so the third harmonic has
the ratio 3/1 and the fifth harmonic has the ratio 5/1. these pitches are
actually quite high relative to the fundamental, so if we want to use the
pitches to make music, it is helpful to put them in the same octave. recall
that the ratio of an octave is 2/1. we can write the ratio 1/1 to mean a
single pitch, and the pitch an octave above that as 2/1. so, basically if we
have any ratio x/y, if 1/1 <= x/y <= 2/1, then the ratio x/y is within that
octave. to make it easy, if you were to punch in x÷y into a calculator, if the
answer were between 1 and 2, then the pitch is within the octave we are
looking at.

so we can see that the third harmonic, 3/1, and fifth harmonic, 5/1, are not
in the same octave, they are in higher octaves. let us octave reduce them so
that we can more easily understand how the pitches relate to our fundamental.

since we need to bring them down octaves, we will multiply by 1/2 (i.e.,
divide by 2). 3/1*1/2 = 3/2. 3/2 is between 1/1 and 2/1, so it is now in the
octave we are looking at.

now with the fifth harmonic: 5/1*1/2 = 5/2; this is greater than 2/1, so we
need to bring it down another octave. 5/2*1/2 = 5/4. now this is in the octave
we are looking at.

now we have four ratios, 1/1, 3/2, 5/4, and 2/1. lets see what the frequencies
these ratios have when our fundamental is 100Hz. to do that, we simply
multiply, just as we found the frequency of the octave (100Hz*2/1=200Hz).

so 100Hz*3/2=150Hz and 100Hz*5/4=125Hz. we can check are work: since 3/2 is an
octave below 3/1, 150Hz*2=300Hz, shows that 3/2 is an octave below the third
harmonic. likewise, 5/4 is two octaves below the fifth harmonic, so
125Hz*2*2=500Hz.

if we could play these pitches, they would be quite familiar to most
musicians. if we played 150Hz and 100Hz together, this is very close to the
“perfect fifth”. if we played 125Hz and 100Hz together, this is very close to
the “major third”. i say “very close” because nowadays most people would
understand “perfect fifth” and “major third” in relationship to 12-tone equal
temperament, but the “perfect fifth” and “major third” of 12tet are not
exactly these relationships.

however, the ratios 3/2 and 5/4 are just ratios. as you can see, they relate
pitches together by whole numbers, and those numbers are very small, and give
very pleasing sounds. if we must name then, we can call 3/2 a “just perfect
fifth” and 5/4 a “just major third”.5

these ratios form the basis of music of western music, essentially. and their
equal-tempered versions form the basis of most actual western music.

they form the basis because basically all the other intervals, or ratios, that
we commonly use are combinations of these two ratios 3/2 and 5/4. and 2/1 of
course, because most music use pitches in multiple octaves

(yes, we can use the seventh harmonic, eleventh harmonic, thirteenth harmonics
as well, beyond, even, though this is the subject of modern music in just
intonation. most of the music of the world has kept to ratios based on 1, 2,
3, and 5)

#### pythagorean tuning

pythagoras really liked the number 3, so he based a whole tuning system on the
number 3, specifically on the ratio 3/2. what he did was start with a
fundamental, 1/1, and go up and down by 3/2 ratios until he had approximately
12 pitches, which are in fact very similar to the pitches we use today, though
different in their specifics.

as we do this exercise, you might be reminded of the circle of fifths.
however, the ratio 3/2 does not form a circle, it forms an infinite spiral up
and down, because the ratio 3/2, taken up or down, no matter how many times,
will never form an octave of any previous pitch.6

##### two unfortunate digressions

before we do this exercise we need to make two unfortunate digressions.

the first concerns names of notes. there is something magical about the system
of using the letters A,B,C,D,E,F,G as well as sharp (#) and flat (b). it works
really well when applied to just intonation and the two just tuning systems i
will be showing you. im not entirely sure how to explain why, but it just
does, as you will see; it is very useful.

one important thing to be aware of though, if you are only used to thinking of
note names relative to equal temperament: in 12tet we say that C# and Db are
“enharmonic”. the black key just to the right of C on a piano represents both
notes. this is not the case in just intonation. in the two tuning systems i
will be showing you, C# and Db are different pitches! likewise, F# and Gb are
different, E and Fb are different, B# and C are different! in just intonation,
a different name gives a different pitch.

the second important digression: when we look at pitches as a ratio, sometimes
it can be hard to tell how two different ratios are related; which one is
higher? are they close together or far apart? we could convert them to Hz,
based on a specific value for 1/1. for example, say 1/1=100Hz and we want to
look at the ratios 5/4 and 81/64. in this case 100Hz*5/4=125Hz and
100Hz*81/64=126.56Hz. you can see these two ratios are actually pretty close,
but that is hard to see just by the ratios.

at the same time, if we want to compare, it would be annoying to have to
convert to Hz all the times, especially since the 1/1 may change. the
frequency of 5/4 relative to 300Hz will be different than when related to
100Hz.

so we are going to use a system called “cents”. this system is very useful,
and also unfortunate is some ways. it is unfortunate because it is based on
12-tone equal temperament, so when we look at ratios in terms of cents, 12tet
intervals have whole numbers, and just ratios have weird numbers, making it
seem like the just ratios are weird. but this is because the system of cents
was devised based on 12tet. so the space of one octave is 1200cents, and
thereby each half-step is 100cents. so if C is 1/1, then equal-tempered E is
four half-steps, or 400cents above C.

the formula for converting a ratio to cents will be in this footnote7. as an
example, the two ratios above is cents are as follows, 5/4=386.3 cents;
81/64=407.8 cents. so remember that the equal-tempered major third (C to E,
for example) was 400 cents. you can see that these two just ratios are very
close to the equal-tempered major third. you can also see what i say this is
unfortunate. to me the ratio 5/4 sounds very good and the equal-tempered major
third doesnt sound good at all…but 400 appears like a nicer number than
386.3…but, again, that is simply because cents was devised based on 12tet.
when later we look at 12tet, you will see that it cant truly be described
using ratios.

a 12tet half-step is actually quite a large interval. most humans can probably
distinguish between pitches 10 cents apart. that is, if i played two pitches
that were 10 cents apart, most people would be able to hear that they are
different. even pitches 5 cents apart can be distinguished. people who have
good ears, or good training, like piano tuners and others can distinguish 2,
and sometimes even 1, cent differences. 12tet actually leaves out a lot of
pitch possibilities!

#### 5-limit tuning

the term 5-limit means that we will be using only the 2nd, 3rd, and 5th
harmonics to construct our tuning system.

pythagorean tuning can be considered a 3-limit tuning system, because it uses
only the 2nd and 3rd harmonics. remember that 3/1 is the third harmonic, which
we octave reduce to 3/2. when we go up by a 3/2 ratio from 3/2, that is 9/4.
9/1 is technically the ninth harmonic of 1/1. but it is also the third
harmonic of 3/2, octave-reduced. basically, we constructed a tuning system
using the third harmonic of a bunch of pitches. we found the third harmonic of
1/1, then them third harmonic of that, etc. when we divide by 3/2, we are
finding the pitch for which 1/1 is a third harmonic. thus, C is the third
harmonic of F.

as you can see, when we only use the 3rd harmonic, the ratios become very
large very quickly, and while these large ratios are strictly speaking “in
tune”, because the two pitches of the ratio can be related by whole numbers,
most of them dont really sound that great.

so let us include the fifth harmonic. we will start by taking the fifth
harmonic of 1/1, then finding the pitch for which 1/1 is the fifth harmonic.
then we can combine the third and fifth harmonics, i.e., find the fifth
harmonic of the third harmonic, the third harmonic of the fifth harmonic, etc.
and see if we can find a pleasant sounding tuning system.

since note names work really well with this system we will use them to help
us. in terms of note names the 5/4 ratio is a “major third”, e.g, C-E, D-F#,
and the 3/2 is a “perfect fifth”, e.g, C-G, D-A.

so let us start with C=1/1. we will be keeping G=3/2 and F=4/3, because these
are the simplest ratios in that system, even simpler than 5/4. we may or may
not keep some other ratios from the 3-limit system, depending on what we find.

so going up from C=1/1 by 5/4 gives E=5/4=386 cents. this is a much simpler
ratio than 81/64, so we will keep this.

going down from 1/1 by 5/4 gives us 4/5. let us bring that into the octave we
are working in by multiplying by 2, giving us Ab=8/5. that is much simpler
than 128/81, so we will keep Ab=8/5.

what if we find the fifth harmonic of the fifth harmonic of 1/1?
5/4*5/4=25/16, which we can call G#. this is definitely better than the
3-limit G#=6561/4096, but we are only wanting to find a 12 note system at this
point, and since G# and Ab inhabit approximately the same space, we are going
to use the Ab we already found.

now what about below? 8/5*4/5*=32/25 which is Fb. this is a fine ratio, but we
are not needing an Fb at the moment.

if we go up again by 5/4 from G# we get B#, and down again from Fb we get Dbb,
neither of which is in our system at the moment. so let us start combining
third and fifth harmonics now.

take 3/2 and go up by 5/4: 3/2*5/4=15/8, G up a 5/4 is B. so B=15/8. our
3-limit B is 243/128. what if we take 5/4 and go up a 3/2? up a fifth from E
is B, so it is the same. so the fifth harmonic of the third harmonic is the
same as the third harmonic of the fifth harmonic, because 3*5=5*3. so as long
we are going in the same direction, the order doesnt matter.

now let us go down by a third harmonic and a fifth harmonic.
1/1÷3/2÷5/4=1/1*2/3*4/5=8/15, which is less than 2, so multiply by 2 to bring
it up an octave gives 16/15. C down a fifth is F, down a third is Db, so
Db=16/15. this is a much better ratio than 256/243.

how about up a 3/2 and down a 5/4? 3/2*4/5=12/10=6/5. up a fifth and down a
major third gives C to G to Eb. so we will take Eb=6/5.

down a 3/2 and up a 5/4: 2/3*5/4=10/12=5/6, 5/6*2=10/6=5/3. down a fifth is F,
up a major third is A. so we will take A=5/3.

here is what we currently have then

as you can see, our ratios are much simpler than most in the 3-limit system
and these intervals will sound very nice.

#### is there a problem with this?

if this were being explained by a western musician or music theorist, at this
point they would probably say something like, “but, wait, theres a problem”.

so, is there a problem with just intonation?

that depends on your goals, your aesthetic, musical values, and to some degree
on your equipment. there is no inherent problem with just intonation.

indian classical music, which is a vast and intricate system, in based on just
intonation. i hope someday to learn about this music from a practitioner, and
to learn how the indians understand intonation, but i will describe my limited
understanding.

the basic instrument of indian classical music is the tanpura, this beautiful
instrument here:

as we know, in order to play music in tune, with whole number ratios, we need
to have certain pitch, a 1/1, to which to tune all of our other pitches.

that is the function of the tanpura is indian classical music. the 1/1 is
called ṣaḍja8, or _sa_ for sort. this is the same idea as the “tonic” in
western music, or the “key” that one is in.

however, indian classical music doesnt “change keys” within the space of one
piece. their system is based on ragas, which is a complex topic. a raga is
often described as a “scale”, which may have different patterns ascending and
descending, but this is only the most simplistic explanation. unfortunately i
do not truly know more about it than it.

the tanpura usually has four strings, with the middle two tuned to 2/1, an
upper _sa_ , the lowest string tuned to 1/1 _sa_ , and then the first may be
tuned to different notes depending on the raga, but most often it will be 3/2,
the “dominant”, pañcam or _pa_.

the indian system of note names is a moveable solfeggio. so _sa_ is always the
tonic, _pa_ is always the dominant. therefore, it doesn’t particular matter
what the frequency of the 1/1 _sa_ is; the performer can choose based on the
instrument, or his or her voice.

the tanpura is really a magical instrument. it is constructed and played in
such a way that the harmonics can be clearly heard. what is more, a skilled
tanpura player can tune the instrument in such a way that the overtones that
one hears match very well with the pitches the solo instrument is playing. you
can read more about the specifics of how tanpura works
[here](https://puretones.sadharani.com/learn/tanpuraworking-1/).

so just intonation is not inherently problematic, and in fact the whole
tradition just described is based on just intonation.

however, there are so uses for which just intonation can be challenging, and
this most often comes down to three facts:

  1. wanting to “change key” in the middle of a piece of music, i.e., wanting to change the pitch that is used to define all of the other pitches

  2. wanting to regularly play multiple pitches at once, i.e, chords, and have complex harmonies

  3. being limited by instrument design

some people will say that indian classical music doesnt have harmony, which i
disagree with. the tanpura always has at least two pitches and the performer
will be most often sounding a third pitch. in fact, harmony at its most basic
is how intonation works. also consider that the fundamental, fifth harmonic,
and third harmonic together make a just major triad, so even one string of a
tanpura by itself can sound a major chord.

however, it is truly that complex harmony, and using chords based on different
scale degrees is not part of this system.

just intonation itself is not necessarily an issue for complex harmony, but
thats were number 3 comes in. somewhere along the line in europe someone made
a keyboard with 12 notes per octave, and in some sense this is what really
limits just intonation in western music.

when we get a keyboard with 12 notes per octave, and we want to change keys,
and we want complex harmony all together, things become difficult. let us
consider why.

let us say we have a harpsichord that is tuned to our 5-limit scale in this
manner:

[![](https://substack-post-
media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/45a38788-b7f7-4639-a0fa-75932cc40e31_882x114.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!n19-!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-
post-
media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F45a38788-b7f7-4639-a0fa-75932cc40e31_882x114.jpeg)

then we know we can play a major chord based on C. what about F? we can find
the difference between a ratio by dividing the higher by the lower. so what is
the distance between A and F, which we want to be a just major third? 5/3÷4/3
=> 5/3*3/4 = 15/12 = 5/4, which is a just major third, and we know F to C is a
3/2.

we also know G to B is 5/4, since that is how we found B, and that G to D is a
3/2. so we have the chords I, IV, and V. what about ii, a minor chord based on
D? F to D => 4/3*8/9 = 32/27, which is a 3-limit minor third, acceptable. what
about D to A? 5/3*8/9 =40/27=680 cents. oh! we want D to A to be 3/2 in order
to make a chord, but D to A is 22 cents smaller than a 3/2. while 40/27 is
technically in tune, it doesnt sound very good. it is called a “wolf-fifth”.
basically what this means is that with a keyboard instrument with this tuning,
we cannot reasonably play a minor ii chord.

suppose we want to modulated to the V for a bit, and in doing so we wanted to
use the dominant chord in the dominant, so a major chord based on D. well, we
know D to F# is a 5/4 ratio, but again, D to A is 40/27, so this is not
viable.

so this is the issue: with only twelve keys, if we tune to a just scale, we
have very limited options for what other keys we could change to; well, none
others actually, since even in the key of 1/1, you dont have all the chords
based on the scale degrees.

we could build an instrument that has many more keys than just 12 so that we
can add extra pitches. then we could make a key for 3/2 ratio above D, call it
A+, and then we could have our ii and dominant-dominant chord. and we could
keep adding keys until we had all the pitches we wanted.

in fact, a 16-century inventor actually tried something like this, though it
wasn’t exactly based on just intonation (you can watch a 12tone video about it
[here](https://youtu.be/INW7IORhqT0?si=xmYRvKY2ilnEcMu8)). obviously that
didn’t catch on, and over the centuries many other people have tried a wide
variety of things in order to make keyboards, chords, and key-changing work
much better with just intonation. alas, to no avail. the 12 key system, i
suspect, is simply too convenient…and difficult enough to use in practice that
adding more keys probably wouldnt be very pleasant.

so what do we do if we want to change keys and play complex harmonies with 12
keys?

this is where the idea of temperament comes in. in temperament, we “temper”,
i.e., slightly change, our intervals from the just ratios to allow for greater
flexibility. so a tempered interval is not technically in tune, but is there a
way it can be tempered that sounds acceptable and not too terrible? some
people think so.

for example, meantone temperaments keep the 5/4 major third, but slightly
reduce the 3/2 ratio. on a keyboard tuned in this way, one can play in about
six keys. in should be noted that in meantone temperament we still do not have
“enharmonic” notes, i.e, in meantone, C# and Db are different. the consequence
of these two things is that there is usually an interval, often G# to Eb,
which is a fifth in theory, but in practice is a “wolf-fifth”. there are many
different varieties of meantone, but they all follow this basic idea.

then there are the so-called well-temperaments. this name comes from bach’s
_well-tempered clavier_. “tempered” here means that it is not in just
intonation, which bach surely knew all about. some have suggested that well-
temperament was 12-tone equal temperament, but i doubt it, in accord with kyle
gann. one reason is that there is another way to temper the tuning that allows
one to use all twelves notes in both major and minor keys, yet isnt equal
temperament.

essentially, whereas meantone temperament changed all the fifths, well-
temperament changes only some of the fifths. that is, some of the fifths are
3/2 ratios, while some are not. the consequence is that some major thirds are
5/4 ratios, while some are not. however, properly done, all the keys have
triads that are “bearable”, i.e., that sound good enough to make music with
them.

often people will speak of different keys having different feelings. i am
dubious of this in 12tet, because, as we will see shortly, in 12tet,
regardless of which key, all intervals have the same relationship with each.
of course, for people like jacob with perfect pitch, this makes sense, but for
people like me, who cant tell the difference between F and A, for example,
without reference, it doesnt make sense.

however, in well-temperament is makes perfect sense that some keys would have
a different quality than other keys, because they literally are different. one
key has a justly tuned triad, another key does not.

im guessing that most recordings of the _well-tempered clavier_ today are in
12tet. i would love to hear one in a well-temperament.

that brings us to the final step on our journey, our standing tuning system in
modern western music:

#### 12 tone equal temperament

the idea of equal temperament is that we take the octave and split it into an
certain number of equal parts. since the 12-keyed keyboard had long been
standard, 12 equal parts seemed to be the thing to try. of course, you can
split the octave into however many equal parts as you want using the same
method.

in 12tet, there are 12 “half-steps”. let us first try taking our 5-limit
“half-step”, 16/15, and see what happens when we go up by 12 of them. since we
multiply to go up by a certain interval, we are going to multiply 16/15 by
itself 12 times. in other words

which turns out to be 1341 cents, which is quite a bit more than one octave.
so 12 5-limit half-steps is more than an octave, but that does show us how we
can find the number we want

i wrote 2 instead of 2/1 for convenience. this equation asks, what number _x_
, when multiplied by itself 12 times equals 2? i.e., what ratio/interval, when
we go up by the interval twelves times, equals an octave. and that interval is
2^(1/12), or the 12th root of 2. please notice that this is not technically a
ratio, because a ratio compares two whole numbers, and no _n_ th root of 2 for
_n_ between 1 and 12 inclusive can be written as the relationship of two whole
numbers.

but this is in fact the number that 12tet is based on. here is the whole scale
based on this irrational number

youll notice that now we have “enharmonic” notes. whereas in just intonation
and meantone temperament, D# is different from Eb, here in 12tet we call them
by the same name, and they have the same pitch, because we have intentionally
limited the division of the octave to 12 notes. whereas in the other systems,
just intonation in particular, there are actually an infinite number of notes.

if we want to find the frequency, we do the same as with ratios.

the wavy equals sign means “approximately”, since 2^(1/12) actually has
infinitely many numbers after the decimal point.

so if we tune A4=440Hz, then A#/Bb = 466.1637615180899Hz, etc.

this is where the use of cents is unfortunate. cents was basically designed
for 12tet, so E is 400 cents, and F is 500 cents, etc., which makes it seem
like these are nice intervals…but they arent really.

the advantage of 12tet, for those who want it, is that one can change keys at
will and play complex harmony…if you think the sound is bearable. apparently,
most people think the sound is reasonable, and find something to like in an
equal-tempered triad and any equal-tempered chord you can name or invent.

i, however, do not.

#### temperament deaf

i dont think 12tet sounds very good. once i was exposed to the beauty of a
just triad, which sounds very good and right to me, 12tet just doesnt have it.
of course, i listen to music in 12tet, because it is almost impossible not to
nowadays, though i try not to listen too closely. its not so much that is
sounds “out of tune”, it simply has a quality that i find grating. piano and
guitars, especially when playing chords, sound very bad to me. it is really a
qualitative dislike, rather than an awareness of specific pitches being out of
tune. i can hear generally patterns in a harmony, but my ears dont do well
with multiple notes played at once, especially complex chords…i just cant make
sense of them sonically.

one of the possible reasons it doesnt sound good to me is that my ears might
be more sensitive to upper partial tones. as we know, any pitch with harmonic
upper partial tones actually contains a major triad, and if we play a just
major triad then those harmonics are reinforced by congruent fundamentals,
which also have harmonics that align well with all of the pitches.

if we play C-E-G on a 12tet piano, the C will produce a major triad in its
harmonics, but the E that is played **wont** be in line with the C’s
harmonics. same for the G. so whereas in just intonation harmonics align, in
12tet harmonics do not align, and this i believe is one reason that i do not
appreciate 12tet.

so that is the story of why i thought i was tone deaf, and how i discovered
that i am simply temperament deaf, and can, in fact, appreciate natural
intonation.

#### further resources

some good books about tuning theory

 _The Arithmetic of Listening_ by Kyle Gann - i learned primarily from this
book. gann talks about just intonation as well as equal temperament, as well
as related systems. there are some systems that are called equal-divisons-of-
the-octave (edos). they are similar to equal temperament in that they divide
the octave into equal parts, but they dont use all of the possible notes. the
idea is approximate just intonation, for instance, if you use 53 or 55 equal
divisions of the octave, there are some intervals that are very close to just
intervals. this book is a really good introduction to tuning theory.

_Genesis of a Music_ by Harry Partch - partch was a truly american composer.
he was born in 1901 and was something of piano virtuoso in his youth, until he
found _on the sensations of tone_ by helmholtz, which introduced him to just
intonation. not long after he decided to only write music in just intonation,
and consequently he built many of his own instruments in order to play the
music he devised. partch’s [original
instruments](https://youtu.be/9UZjhTlGT0o?si=5-cm3hvtSw3M9PIi) are housed at
the university of washington in seattle, where they are still played. there
are two other sets of copies of his instruments that have been built and
performed upon.

_On the Sensations of Tone_ by Hermann von Helmholtz. this is a scientific
exposition of the upper partial tones. a very thorough account of experiments
into the nature of sound, with large sections on tuning theory.
